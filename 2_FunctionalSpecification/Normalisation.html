Booking Table Normalisation

The above figure shows the normalisation of the Booking Table. Up to 3 normalisation forms are produced. I have taken all the attributes that are related to bookings and input them into the un-normalised form.  Within these attributes there are repeating and un-repeating data. The repeating data are as following,  

  • BookingID

  • GuestID

  • RoomID

  • ExtraChargeID

In the 1st normalisation form I have separated the attributes that are related to Booking details, Room details and Guest detail and entered them into the right entities. I have set ‘BookingID’ as the primary key in the Booking Details Table, ‘RoomID’ as the primary key in the Room Details Table and ‘GuestID’ as the primary key in the Guest Details Table.

As you can see that in the above figure the attributes that are coloured in red are shown as the primary key in that particular table.

In the 2nd normalisation form I have looked closely into the Room Details Table and separated the attributes that are related to Extra charge details and entered them into the right entity. The ‘ExtraChargeID’ is taken from the Booking Details Table and I have set the ‘ExtraChargeID’ as the primary key. 

3rd normalisation form, by analysing the tables I have found that the attributes related to booking cancelation details can be separated from the Booking Details Table. So I have taken the attributes and entered them into the right entity. I have set the ‘BookingCancelationID’ as the primary key. 

You can see that all the attributes are separated into their right entities and under one unique primary key.

Payment Table Normalisation

The above figure shows the normalisation of the Payment Table. Up to 3 normalisation forms are produced. I have taken all the attributes that are related to payments and input into the un-normalised form.  Within these attributes there are repeating and un-repeating data. The repeating data are as following, 

  • BookingID

  • GuestID

  • RoomID

  • ExtraChargeID

  • PaymentID

1st normalisation form, by analysing all the attribute in the un-normalised form I have separated the data that are related to Payment Details, Booking Details, Room Details and Guest detail and entered them into their entities. By setting ‘PaymentID’ as the primary key in the Payment Details Table, ‘BookingID’ as the primary key in the Booking Details Table, ‘RoomID’ as the primary key in the Room Details Table and ‘GuestID’ as the primary key in the Guest Details Table.  

The Primary keys are coloured in red and can be identified easily in the above figure.

2nd normalisation form. By looking closely into the Room Details Table, I separated the attributes that are related to Extra charge details and entered them into their entity. I have set the ‘ExtraChargeID’ as the primary key. 

Lastly 3rd normalisation form. By analysing the Payment Details Table in the 2nd normalised form, I found that there are attributes that can be taken out and entered into a separate table. I took out the attributes ‘PaymentID’, ‘BookingID’, ‘GuestID’, ‘RoomID’, ‘ExtraChargeID’ and ‘TotalCost’ and entered them into the Pay Update Table. I have not set a primary key to this because this table is only used for updating. There won’t be any data entry.

The User Details Table

I have decided not to normalise this table because there is no relationship forming from the User Details Table with other tables. The User Details Table itself is a separate and an individual table. I analysed that this table is already in the 3rd normalised form. For this reason only I have decided not to normalise this table.

Final normalised form

As you can see all the attributes are separated into suitable tables with a unique primary key.
